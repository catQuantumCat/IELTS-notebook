People think that the fuel for personal vehicles should be increased by the government as an attempt solve to the environmental problems. While it is true that this can be an effective solution in the short term, I would argue that a well-rounded plan can even make bigger impacts.

If authorities decide to raise the tax of fuel, those who own cars or motorbikes would have to pay more to keep their personal means of transport on the road. Because of that, more and more people would be incentivised to switch over to public transportation as an economic alternative. Furthermore, the tax surpluses collected from the individual commuters can be invested to improve aspects of public transportation - better planning and infrastructures, newer and more efficient technologies, and more. This, in turn, can ultimately lower fares of using buses, trams, subways, etc.

On the other hand, a significant price increase in a short amount of time is not going to be an all-in-one solution. Instead, solutions to reduce the green house impacts from personal vehicles should be backed by scientific research, and applied methodically. One of such successful approach is the concept of low emission zones - areas considered to have high traffic flows and low air quality. Vehicles entering such regions must be compliant to certain emission standards, or owners of those transports must pay a hefty price to enter for only certain hours of the day. First tested in London, this way has discouraged the use of private transportation among locals, and is now applied to hundreds of cities globally. Another city in Europe - Amsterdam - is well-known for its cycling culture. This is a result of decades-long planning and experimenting on sustainable traffic infrastructure. The Netherlands' capital city is now the one with the majority of its road built specifically for biking, and sedans or SUVs are rarely to be seen there.

In conclusion, a heavy taxation on gas can - to an extent - be able to guide people to use greener transportation. But to fundamentally change what type of transport people choose to commute, it is the well thought-out official regulations and modern concepts that will help in the long-term effort to combat global warming.
