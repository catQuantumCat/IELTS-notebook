Some people think that crime results from outside circumstances, while others argue that it is because of genetic properties. Despite both views having their own logic—which will be discussed in this essay—I firmly agree with the former.  
  
On the one hand, it is believed that the bedrock for all social evils lies in humans' innate properties. This school of thought underestimates the law while suggesting that genetic editing technology is the primary solution. For example, some people born with certain mental disorders are unable to distinguish their actions as good or bad. Meanwhile, research conducted on serious crime offenders shows that they share the same DNA mutation. This abnormality is said to be responsible for their high tendency to commit crimes. However, those studies are argued to be highly biased, with test subjects being handpicked.  
  
On the other hand, the majority of the public agrees with the effects that external circumstances can have on potential law-breakers. This is based on the fact that humans' moral values are not fixed but subject to change. To demonstrate, some juvenile crimes—pickpocketing or shoplifting—are often done by teenagers with abusive childhoods, grown up with financial burdens, or not provided with adequate education. In contrast, felons having spent years behind bars have converted into lawful citizens with steady income, thanks to rehabilitation programs. Moreover, this perspective informs lawmakers' and law enforcers' actions against crime. Therefore, by recognizing and adjusting outside situations, we can actively change one's characteristics, for the better.  
  
In conclusion, while there are valid arguments protecting both views, I'm in favor of the last opinion. I have faith in the people's willingness to overcome difficult issues. With help from society, all of us deserve to be decent human beings.
