Some people argued that it is best for regulators to allocate the resources into their future leaders, as a way to be prepared for the future. While I do recognize the potential of this group in shaping the tomorrow's world, I think that we should not neglect other aspects of a country.

At a governmental administrative level, young officers who earned attention and support from the nation can seriously transform a country. Viet Nam is among the prime examples for this, having their slow and rigid public service's workforce led by those in their 20s, significantly improving the productivity, speed and quality. As for the private sector, workers can be equipped with mental and physical capabilities from the nation's priority in adacemic and health growth. This attracts international capital to the domestic market. All of these are to point out that investments in the young generation are beneficial to all aspects of a country.

However, this is not to say that other resources are less important to be invested in. In contrast, a futureproof plan from lawmakers should be comprehensive - one that opens opportunities for the future leaders, while not compromising the current citizen's welfare. Investments in public transportation modes, eco-friendly energy sources and free public health systems are the bedrocks to both the current and future residents. Regions in the EU have been able to pull this off, with excellent public services and legislation that focuses on long-term growth rather than short-term benefit. As a number of young Nobel Prize winners are from this region, it is safe to say that the young simply cannot support any nation if it is not a good place to live.

In conclusion, investments in the young generations are crucial enhancing national political and financial status. Yet other faces of the society are also in need of being taken care of, to secure a well-rounded growth.
