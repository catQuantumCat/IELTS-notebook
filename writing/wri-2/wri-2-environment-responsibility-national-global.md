It is argued that global agencies - not individual governments - should be responsible for finding solutions to the current ecological challenges. While the actions from the former are significant, I think this subject requires efforts from both bodies.

It is essential to highlight the role of international organizations in incentivising knowledge exchange. In particular, these forums encourage countries to transfer findings and technologies of combating global warming. Shared resources like academic results on the effect of microplastics have on different ocean areas, or the invention of a more affordable wind turbine can really speed up the process of reverting climate change. Moreover, it is crucial to have NGOs and even other countries govern one's process in executing joint climate agreements. This aspect helps ensure transparency and viability of reaching the common goal. From those, we can see that actions from international organizations in this matter cannot be underestimated.

However, this is not to say that national governments are outside of this trend. As these bodies possess natural and economical resources, they also bear the responsibility to put a stop to environmental degradation. Viet Nam has been one of the highlights in this, by pulling out national transformation to reach the UN's carbon neutral goal in 2050. More specifically, this developing country spent millions of dollars in renewable energy sources, and made recyclable paper bags more appealing to citizens through tax relief. These steps have been recognised by the international communities to be effective and promising. By utilising taxation and political power, nations are expected to align global agreements with actionable items while securing social welfare.

I firmly believe that with great power comes great responsibility. This is true for both international and domestic governing entities. The common effort to save our planet from the detrimental consequences of climate change should be done, regulated and shared by all.
