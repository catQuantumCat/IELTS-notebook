It is argued that it should be the government's responsibility - not companies' – to carry out scientific research. I fully support this point of view, as it ensures the results from academic experiments or surveys are intended for enhancing people's lives – and not reserved for a selected group.

To start, having scientific exploration activities be sponsored by the private sector means that only a limited group of people benefit from these outputs. These entities spend on technical R&D and social research as a way to increase their profit margin. Take research papers about dehydration produced by in-house laboratories of bottled-water manufacturers, for instance. The data and method of investigation have been carefully peer-reviewed. Yet the conclusion deceptively leads consumers to think that they are not drinking enough water daily. This, in turn, helps boost the sales of bottled water by fabricating a false need. It is their profitability that incentivises them to do research, which is not guaranteed to be done in good faith.

On the other hand, I firmly believe that publicly funded academic projects are for the welfare of the citizens. A proportion of money from taxpayers is allocated for tackling realistic problems, such as illiteracy, poverty and social evils – which are faced by people from all walks of life, especially those from marginalised groups. Having those problems solved means that residents are given more supports to escape financial burdens and participate in the job market, which will help better develop a nation. This win-win relationship explains why millions of dollars are being spent on research activities as the stepping stone for improving public transportation, renovating old buildings and many other aspects.

In short, studies carried out by companies are often beneficial to only a selected group. In contrast, more demographics in the society will be able to earn the achievements that state-sponsored research yields. Because of that, I am strongly in favour of the latter view.

Many people believe that it is the government's responsibility to conduct scientific research, as opposed to the private sector. I'm strongly in favour of this belief, as company-sponsored experiments can be used against consumers, while those backed by authorities are done so with the citizens' interest in mind.

To start, corporations are seen to pour money into their in-house labs to run experiments. These tests produce metrics and graphs that despite being accurate, are deliberately presented in a way that creates artificial demands. For instance, beverage manufacturers experienced sky-rocketing profits after their research produced conclusions about how most people are underhydrated, and gave advice on drinking at least 2 liters of water per day. As these results have been debunked by scholars, this is to show that advancements in science can be a powerful tool for companies to control consumer behaviours.

On the other hand, scientific studies sponsored by official authorities are carried out to satisfy social needs. Finding solutions for those problems through research - aided by taxation and favourable legal frameworks - is crucial for the social welfare and nationwide economical growth. To illustrate, residents from To Lich river in central Hanoi, Vietnam for years had suffered from an unhygienic living condition, resulting from a degraded wastewater treatment system. To tackle this, the Ministry of Environment poured money into universities' labs in search of a sustainable solution. Out of many submitted, one was chosen for deployment, and the quality of life around this area has increased dramatically. That is to say, social classes benefit from achievements from government-backed research.

In conclusion, outputs from private companies' labs are often presented in a misleading way, as to artificially increase profit. In contrast, those of the public sector - supported by legislations and government budget - are to serve the civilian's needs, and should be prioritised.
