The table gives information about the proportion of four different sources of electricity in five Asian nations, in 2005.  

Overall, it is noticeable that South Korea and Japan were heavily dependent on eco-friendly sources. The opposite can be said for the other three countries, which mostly utilised non-environmentally friendly fuel types.

Green energy sources were dominant in Japan and South Korea. More specifically, nearly half of the electricity in Japan was powered by nuclear plants, with that of South Korea standing at 33%.  On the other hand, while less than 20% of electricity was created using this source in Singapore and Malaysia, it was not available in Thailand. Another renewable energy source -  hydro and wind power - produced 48% of the electricity in South Korea, and 19% in Thailand However, it did not account for much of the energy production in other countries, generating at most 5% of the total electricity per each.

Regarding non-renewable fuel and other categories, the three South East Asian countries relied significantly on these. Coal and lignite were responsible for 61% and 42% of electricity produced in Malaysia and Singapore. Meanwhile, Thailand preferred using petroleum products, with its percentage at 36%. In contrast, the two nations from East Asia used these fuel types considerably less, with no more than 6% used. Notably; Thailand, Singapore and Japan were recorded to have approximately 40% of their electricity from other sources
